# SOCCER DAD
## Evermore Life — Hero Video Script
**Format:** Cinematic narrative | Runtime: 5:30–6:00 minutes
**Production:** AI video generation (Sora/Runway) + HeyGen spokesperson + ElevenLabs voiceover
**Version:** 1.0 — Production Draft

---

## PRODUCTION NOTES

**Visual Style:**
Warm, desaturated film look — think early Terrence Malick. Golden hour light dominates. Natural color grading, slightly lifted blacks. No sharp edges. Everything feels like a memory.

**Audio Style:**
Sparse piano underscore through acts 1–3. Swells of orchestral strings at the emotional peak. Silence is used intentionally — hold beats where other brands would cut away. The voiceover is a soft male voice, 40s–50s, unhurried. Think Morgan Freeman register without the affectation.

**Pacing:**
Slow. Trust the visuals. Do not rush. This is not a commercial — it's a short film that happens to end with a brand.

**On-Screen Text:**
Minimal. White, serif font (Georgia or Freight Display). Appears and disappears quietly. No animations — just fade in, fade out.

### ACT ONE: THE BOY WHO HAD A GIFT — [0:20–1:10]

**[VISUAL]**
A montage — quick, warm, textured. The boy on a soccer field at sunset, running. Silhouetted against an orange sky. The ball seems to move with him like it belongs there. His feet are gifted. You can see it even in these brief frames.

**[VISUAL]**
He's in a locker room, talking with teammates — the kind of easy confidence that athletic ability gives a teenager. Someone hands him a newspaper — his name circled in a sports column.

**[VISUAL]**
He's at home. Simple house. His mother is watching him practice in the backyard, smiling from the window.

**[VOICEOVER — unhurried, reflective]**
"He was the best player in his county. Scouts noticed. His coach told him he had what it took."

**[VISUAL]**
Close-up of cleats — worn, grass-stained, loved.

**[VISUAL]**
He sits with papers spread on a kitchen table. His mother points at something. There's excitement — but also something uncertain in her face.

**[VOICEOVER]**

### ACT TWO: LIFE HAPPENED — [1:10–2:30]

**[VISUAL]**
Hard cut: the same kitchen table, years later. Different. Older. A baby's noise in the background.

**[VISUAL]**
He's older now — early 30s. The soccer player is still there in his posture, but softer. He's in work clothes — not a uniform, not a jersey. He comes through the door tired. Sets down his bag. Loosens his collar.

**[VISUAL]**
His wife is in the kitchen. There's a baby in a carrier on the counter. The baby's hand reaches up. He walks over, and for the first time — his face completely changes. He picks up the baby. The tiredness is gone.

**[VOICEOVER]**
"Life happened. The way it does."

**[VISUAL]**
A brief, wordless montage — the years passing. The father working. Night shifts. Early mornings. A second job, maybe. His hands are callused now. He's good at this life he's built — even if it's not the one he imagined.

**[VISUAL]**
He sits in a beat-up car in a parking lot. It's raining. He's looking at his phone — maybe a bill, maybe something else. He puts the phone down. Looks straight ahead for a moment. Then exhales. And gets back out to go back to work.

**[VOICEOVER]**
"His own dreams got quiet. Not gone — quiet. The way a good man's dreams do when he finds something worth more."

**[VISUAL]**
He comes home late. The house is dark. He checks on his son — small now, maybe 4 — sleeping. He stands in the doorway for a long moment. Just watching.

**[VOICEOVER]**
"He never talked about what he gave up. He was too busy with what he had."

### ACT THREE: THE GIFT PASSES — [2:30–3:45]

**[VISUAL]**
Slow fade in: a backyard. Morning. The son — now 6 or 7 — kicks a ball against a fence. Kick. Thud. Kick. Thud. Not coached. Just instinct.

**[VISUAL]**
The father watches from the back porch, coffee in hand. He doesn't say anything. Just watches. A slow smile starts — but it's not the smile of a man projecting his dreams. It's recognition. *He sees it too.*

**[VOICEOVER]**
"He didn't push it. He would never push it. But when his son found the ball — naturally, the way gifts find people — he recognized it."

**[VISUAL]**
The father sets down his coffee. Walks into the yard. Without a word, he takes the ball. Demonstrates a technique — a proper approach, a clean strike. The son watches. Tries it. The ball goes wide. They both laugh.

**[VISUAL]**
Montage — years of this. Rain or shine. Early mornings before school. Saturday afternoons at the park. The father always on the sideline, never on the field. His role is clear: he is the supporter, not the player. This dream belongs to his son.

**[VISUAL]**
A youth soccer game. The son, now 10, lines up for a penalty kick. The crowd is small — other parents, siblings, some kids on bikes. The father is on the sideline. He's quiet. His hands are clasped. He is completely present.

**[VISUAL — slow motion]**
The son strikes the ball. It curves — perfectly — into the top corner of the net.

**[VISUAL — slow motion]**
The father's reaction. He doesn't scream. He doesn't pump his fist first. His eyes close for just a second. Just one second. Then he opens them. And he claps.

**[VOICEOVER]**
"Every game. Every tournament. Every early morning and late night and muddy Saturday."

**[VISUAL]**
The son — now a teenager — at an elite camp. Other fathers are pointing, coaching, managing. This father is just watching. Proud. Present.

**[VOICEOVER]**
"He didn't do it because he missed his chance. He did it because he loved his son more than he loved his own story."

### ACT FOUR: THE MOMENT — [3:45–4:45]

**[VISUAL]**
A different stadium now. Professional scale. Bright lights. A crowd of thousands. A scoreboard. This is real.

**[VISUAL]**
The son — now 24, 25 — in a professional kit. Warmup. He's scanning the stands. Looking for something. Looking for someone.

**[VISUAL]**
The father in the stands. He's older now — 60s. The years are visible. He's wearing a scarf in his son's team colors. He doesn't have a sign or a foam finger. He just sits there, hands folded in his lap, watching.

**[VISUAL]**
The match unfolds in quick cuts — the energy of it, the son's skill visible even at distance. The crowd ebbs and surges.

**[VISUAL — slow motion]**
The son scores. A stunning goal. The stadium erupts.

**[VISUAL]**
Players pile on him. The celebration is enormous. The cameras find him. He raises a fist. But then — he's looking.

**[VISUAL]**
His eyes find his father in the stands.

**[VISUAL]**
The son breaks from the celebration — gestures to a teammate — and starts walking toward the sideline. Then jogging.

**[VISUAL — slow motion]**
He runs to the railing. His father is standing now. He leans down over the railing and they embrace — the son in his kit, the father in his everyday clothes. It's not elegant. It's real.

**[VISUAL — hold on this for three full seconds]**
The embrace. Neither one lets go first.

**[ON-SCREEN TEXT — slow fade in]**
*"Your legacy moves on."*

**[VISUAL]**
The screen holds on them, slowly brightening, then slowly fading to near-black.

## TECHNICAL SPECS FOR PRODUCTION

**Aspect ratios needed:**
- 16:9 (YouTube, Facebook, desktop)
- 9:16 (Reels/TikTok/Shorts cut)
- 1:1 (Facebook feed square)

**AI Video Generation Approach:**
- Scenes 1–4: Sora or Runway ML for cinematic AI video generation (see AI_VIDEO_PROMPTS.md)
- Act 5 (spokesperson): HeyGen for realistic talking-head segment
- Act 6: Motion graphics (After Effects or Canva Pro)
- Voice: ElevenLabs (use "Daniel" voice or similar — warm male, mid-tone, measured pace)
- Music: Artlist.io search terms: "emotional piano legacy cinematic" — track should build from solo piano to strings by Act 4

**Editing notes:**
- Hold on the embrace (Act 4) for at minimum 3 seconds of silence before music swells
- Color grade: Warm shadows, slightly desaturated highlights — LUT reference: "Cinematic Warm Autumn"
- Do NOT use jump cuts in Acts 1–4. Every edit should be a slow dissolve or a hard cut on a beat.
